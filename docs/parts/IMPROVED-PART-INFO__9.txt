Operating Principle

The measurement process is based on time-of-flight:

Trigger: The microcontroller sends a 10µs HIGH pulse to the HC-SR04's Trig pin.

Emission: The module emits 8 bursts of 40kHz ultrasound.

Reception: The sound waves travel, reflect off an object, and return to the module.

Echo Pulse: The sensor's Echo pin goes HIGH for the duration of the sound's round trip. The width of this pulse corresponds to the time taken.

Calculation: The distance is calculated from the measured pulse width (duration) using the speed of sound.

Distance (cm): duration (µs) / 58

Distance (inches): duration (µs) / 148

Voltage Divider Method: The most common and cheapest solution. A 10k resistor from the Echo pin to a junction, and a 20k resistor from the junction to GND. The output (from the junction) will be approximately 3.3V when the Echo pin is HIGH.

Principle of Operation (AMR Effect): Both the HMC5883L and QMC5883L utilize the Anisotropic Magnetoresistance (AMR) effect to sense magnetic fields[2][9]. An AMR sensor contains a thin strip of ferromagnetic material (like Permalloy, a nickel-iron alloy)[2]. The electrical resistance of this material changes predictably in the presence of an external magnetic field[2]. The IC integrates three of these sensors in a Wheatstone bridge configuration to detect changes in magnetic fields along the X, Y, and Z axes[2]. The output from these sensors is then digitized by an internal Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC)[9].

Heading Calculation: The heading (azimuth angle) is derived from the X and Y axis readings relative to the local magnetic north direction[12]. The calculation uses the atan2(Y, X) function, which takes into account the signs of both X and Y to accurately determine the angle in all four quadrants (0° to 360°)[12]. For full 3D orientation (like tilt-compensation), the Z axis reading is combined with data from an accelerometer to adjust the heading calculation based on pitch and roll[12][13].

Built-in Features: Both chips include features designed to improve accuracy and ease of use. 5. Application Context and Integration

Typical Applications: Digital compassing, navigation systems, drone/UAV orientation, robotics, and personal navigation devices[2][9].

Integration Best Practices:

Magnetic Calibration (Hard Iron & Soft Iron): The single most important step for accurate results. Hard iron distortion is caused by permanent magnetic fields from nearby objects (e.g., motors, power lines, magnets) that create a constant offset[1][14]. Soft iron distortion results from ferromagnetic materials (e.g., iron screws, steel brackets) that distort the magnetic field's shape, causing readings to form an ellipse instead of a perfect circle[1][13][14]. Hard iron calibration involves calculating and subtracting this fixed bias, while soft iron calibration uses a scaling matrix to correct for field distortion[12][13].

Placement Considerations: Mount the magnetometer as far away as possible from sources of magnetic interference, particularly DC motors, large current-carrying wires (especially switching power supplies), and metallic components[1][12]. A distance of at least 10 cm is generally recommended[1].

Without tilt compensation, the X/Y projection of the magnetic field vector on a tilted plane will provide an incorrect reading.

Redundancy: If using a high-level IMU like the Bosch BNO055 or similar 9-DOF modules, a standalone magnetometer is often redundant. The BNO055 integrates a magnetometer with an accelerometer and gyroscope to perform hardware-level sensor fusion and provide a pre-compensated, absolute orientation output[12].

Overcurrent Protection: Disconnects the battery from the load if the draw exceeds a safe limit (typically 15-30A for hoverboard BMS)[10][15].

3. Functional Analysis and Operating Principles

Outrunner BLDC Design
This component utilizes an outrunner design, where the permanent magnets are arranged on the inner face of the rotating outer casing (rotor)[4]. The three-phase windings (stator) are located on the central, fixed axle. This configuration allows for a large rotor diameter and a high number of pole pairs (magnets), which results in high torque output at low speeds[5]. The high torque allows for direct drive without a gearbox, making the system mechanically simple and quiet.

Trapezoidal Commutation using Hall Sensors
Unlike brushed DC motors that use mechanical contacts for commutation, this BLDC motor relies on an electronic controller and Hall sensors to determine when to energize each phase winding[6]. The three Hall effect sensors are positioned to detect changes in the magnetic field as the rotor's magnets pass by them[3].

The motor controller uses a "six-step commutation" (also known as trapezoidal control or 120-degree conduction) sequence. The sensors provide a 3-bit code (High/Low state for each of the three sensors) that changes every 60 electrical degrees of rotation[7]. Based on this code, the controller energizes two of the three phase windings (A, B, C) at any given time, creating a magnetic field that pulls the rotor toward the next position. The high number of pole pairs (15 pairs = 30 magnets) means one mechanical rotation requires 15 electrical rotations. This high pole count translates to high torque at low speed, ideal for hoverboard applications[2][5].

Position Feedback and Gray Code
The Hall sensors are typically positioned 120 electrical degrees apart[3][7]. As the rotor rotates, the sensors generate a repeating sequence of six unique states (a Gray code pattern) per electrical revolution[7]. This pattern allows the controller to precisely determine the rotor's position and switch the phase currents in the correct sequence to maintain continuous rotation. The original documentation's commutation table (Step 1 to 6) is the standard 6-step commutation sequence.

4. Pinout Diagram and I/O Description

The motor has two distinct wiring harnesses: a thick 3-wire cable for the motor phases and a thin 5-wire cable for the Hall effect sensors.

Motor Phase Wires (High Current)

Wire Color	Function	Description
Yellow	Phase A (MA)	High-current connection to Phase A winding.
Green	Phase B (MB)	High-current connection to Phase B winding.
Blue	Phase C (MC)	High-current connection to Phase C winding.

Note on Phase Order: The order in which the phase wires connect to the controller's A, B, and C outputs (MA, MB, MC) determines the direction of rotation relative to the Hall sensor feedback. If the motor runs backward or vibrates violently, two phase wires must be swapped to achieve the correct phase-to-Hall alignment.

Hall Sensor Cable (Low Current/Signal)

Wire Color	Function	Description
Red	VCC (+5V)	Power supply input for the Hall sensors. Must be connected to a stable 5V source from the controller.
Black	GND	Common ground connection for the Hall sensors.
Yellow	Hall A (HA)	Digital output signal from Hall sensor A.
Green	Hall B (HB)	Digital output signal from Hall sensor B.
Blue	Hall C (HC)	Digital output signal from Hall sensor C.

Hall Sensor Output Type: These sensors typically have an open collector output. This requires a pull-up resistor (often 10kΩ) to be present on the controller's input pins to ensure a clean high logic signal[3]. Many commercial controllers (like the riorand-zs-x11h-bldc-controller-6-60v-16a-with-hall-sensor-input) have these pull-up resistors integrated.

5. Application Context and Integration

Typical Applications:

Original Use: Self-balancing scooters (hoverboards).

Salvage Applications: Robotic rovers (4WD, 6WD configurations), electric bicycles, custom electric vehicles, and high-torque linear actuators. The low KV and high torque make them suitable for direct-drive applications where high torque at low speeds is required[8].

Integration Best Practices:

Controller Selection: Use a BLDC controller designed for sensor-based (trapezoidal) commutation and with a voltage rating that supports the 36V nominal voltage (42V peak). The controller's continuous current rating should exceed the motor's anticipated continuous draw (e.g., >10A per motor).

Thermal Management: These motors are designed for short bursts of high power. The solid rubber tire and enclosed design inhibit heat dissipation. For continuous operation at high loads (e.g., in a heavy robot or on inclines), monitor the motor casing temperature. Exceeding approximately 80°C (176°F) can degrade magnets and windings. Consider a controller with thermal protection.

Power Supply Design: A single motor at peak load (15A) requires a battery capable of high discharge rates. A multi-motor system (e.g., 4 motors) can pull 60A peak. The battery pack (like the original 10S hoverboard pack) must have a Battery Management System (BMS) with a peak current rating sufficient for this total load.[9] If using a BMS with a lower rating (e.g., 30A), implement current limiting in the motor controller firmware to prevent BMS overcurrent trips.

Wiring and Safety: Use high-strand silicone wires. For a single motor's peak draw of 15-20A, 14 AWG wire (for short runs) or 12 AWG (for longer runs or multi-motor systems) should be used for the phase connections.[9] Use appropriate fusing between the battery and controller to protect against short circuits (a 20A automotive blade fuse per controller is recommended for single motor use).

6. Troubleshooting and Maintenance

Problem: Motor vibrates violently but does not spin.

Diagnosis: This indicates a mismatch between the Hall sensor sequence and the phase winding connection order[10]. The controller is energizing the incorrect phases for the reported rotor position.

Solution: Swap any two of the three phase wires (Yellow, Green, Blue) connected to the controller. Do not swap all three. This corrects the phase-to-Hall alignment. Test at low voltage/duty cycle first.

Problem: Motor stutters or struggles to start at low speeds, but runs smoothly at high speeds (sensorless fallback).

Diagnosis: One or more Hall sensors are likely failing or have loose connections. The controller's sensor-based commutation relies on all three signals. When a signal is lost, many controllers switch to sensorless mode, which has poor low-speed performance[6].

Solution: Inspect the thin Hall sensor cable for damage, especially at the point where it enters the motor casing. Use a multimeter or oscilloscope to verify the Hall sensor signals. As the wheel is rotated by hand, each Hall output should toggle between 0V and 5V in a specific sequence.

Problem: Motor runs but makes a grinding or scraping noise.

Diagnosis: This is typically a mechanical issue, not electrical. The most common cause is a failed bearing. The motor's outrunner design places stress on the bearings, which can wear out over time.

Solution: Disassemble the motor (often requires removing a circlip or C-clip from the axle) and replace the bearings with new ones (standard bearing sizes can be found in teardowns, typically 6002-ZZ or similar).

Problem: Motor direction is reversed (spins backward from desired).

Diagnosis: The direction of rotation is determined by the phase order.

Solution: Swap any two of the phase wires (e.g., connect Yellow to MC and Blue to MA instead of Yellow to MA and Blue to MC). This reverses the direction without affecting the phase-to-Hall alignment[10].
