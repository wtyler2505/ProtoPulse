KEY FACTS

**The 28BYJ-48 motor:**
- 5V unipolar stepper.
- 5 wires: 4 phase coils + 1 common.
- Phase resistance ~50Ω; current ~100mA per phase at 5V (~240mA per phase peak in some variants).
- Internal motor: 32 steps/revolution.
- Gear reduction: 64:1 (some sources cite 63.68395:1 — the actual ratio is non-integer due to gear teeth count).
- Output shaft: **2048 full-steps/rev** (32 × 64) or **4096 half-steps/rev**.
- Top speed: ~13 RPM unloaded; 5-8 RPM under load. Acceleration limited.

**Why "trades speed for precision":**
- The 64:1 gear reduction kills RPM (motor RPM × 64 ≪ output RPM × 64).
- Excellent for slow precise positioning (clock hands, slow valve actuators, camera-slider macro work).
- USELESS for anything requiring dynamic motion >15 RPM at the output. Use NEMA17 + bipolar driver for those.

**The AccelStepper wiring trap (THE most-bitten gotcha):**
- AccelStepper library expects pins in `IN1, IN2, IN3, IN4` order corresponding to the motor's phase sequence.
- ULN2003 driver board has IN1-IN4 labeled — but the BREAKOUT-TO-MOTOR wiring of the 5-wire connector may permute them.
- Per the vault claim: pin order on AccelStepper is NOT sequential.
- **Symptom of miswire**: motor vibrates and shudders instead of rotating smoothly. Easy to misdiagnose as "broken motor".
- **Fix**: the AccelStepper constructor pin sequence for 28BYJ-48 + ULN2003 is typically `(8, IN1, IN3, IN2, IN4)` — note the IN3/IN2 swap. Verify per board batch.

**Drive modes (the three-way trade):**
- **Wave drive** (1-phase-on): lowest torque, smoothest motion at low speed, simplest. 4 step pattern.
- **Full step** (2-phases-on): higher torque, slightly less smooth, same step count.
- **Half step** (alternating 1 and 2 phase-on): doubles resolution, intermediate torque, smoother. 8 step pattern.
- AccelStepper `Stepper4` mode = half-step. Most projects use this.

**Holding current discipline (battery killer):**
- When the motor is stationary, holding the position requires continuous current through the active phase(s).
- ~100mA continuous when idle = 12 hours from a 1200mAh battery just to hold position.
- **De-energize logic**: when the application doesn't need active holding, set all phases LOW. Motor's internal detent torque holds position approximately.
- For battery projects this is non-negotiable.

**ULN2003 driver:**
- 7-channel Darlington array; only 4 used for 28BYJ-48.
- INs are CMOS/TTL compatible (5V or 3.3V drive logic).
- OUTs sink current to GND (low-side switches).
- Maximum output current: 500mA per channel (well above 28BYJ-48's 240mA needs).
- Includes flyback diodes (clamped to COM pin) for inductive kickback — important; do NOT bypass.
- Module includes 4 LEDs showing phase activation — useful for debugging.

**Stall behavior (open-loop):**
- 28BYJ-48 is open-loop (no encoder, no current sense).
- If torque demand exceeds capability, motor STALLS silently. Position counter in firmware diverges from physical position.
- No recovery without external feedback (limit switch, encoder, hall sensor).
- Mitigation: known reference position (limit switch on power-up homing), or just don't stall (stay below max torque budget).

**Suitable applications:**
- Clock hands.
- Slow camera dollies.
- Plant-watering valve positioners.
- Decorative slow rotation.

**UNSUITABLE applications:**
- 3D printer axes (use NEMA17 + DRV8825/A4988).
- Anything requiring >15 RPM output.
- Anything requiring >40 mNm torque.

ANTI-PATTERNS (NEVER do these)
- Never recommend 28BYJ-48 for >15 RPM applications. Use NEMA17.
- Never wire AccelStepper assuming sequential IN1-IN4. The 28BYJ-48 phase sequence requires the IN2/IN3 swap.
- Never leave the motor energized when not actively positioning on a battery project.
- Never assume position correctness after a missed step. There is NO recovery without external sensing.
